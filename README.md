# Hackathon Guidebook

A practical guidebook for winning hackathons.

This is a collection of years of learnings from competing in hackathons, pushing for real outcomes, losing for the right reasons, and refining the parts that reliably move the result. It is not a theory document and not a highlight reel. It is the operating system behind how we prepare, pick challenges, build under pressure, and pitch in a way that wins.

## What This Is

Hackathons look chaotic from the outside, but the best teams do not win by accident. They manage energy, read the room, choose the right challenge, discover the real case behind the brief, build only what matters, and turn the final pitch into a clear story of value.

The guidebook captures those repeatable patterns in two formats:

- **Learn**: the principles, phases, and decisions behind the approach.
- **Hack**: a phase-by-phase checklist to use during an actual hackathon.

## Who This Is For

This is for people who want to win hackathons.

It is useful whether you are joining your first hackathon or have already done many of them. First-time hackers can use it to avoid obvious traps. Experienced teams can use it to tighten their operating rhythm, reduce duplicated effort, and remember what matters when time pressure gets high.

It is also useful for hackathon organizers, friends, investors, and people in the startup ecosystem who want to understand what strong hackathon execution actually looks like from the inside.

## How To Use It

Start with the **Learn** page. Read through the phases to understand the underlying approach: how to prepare before the event, how to start, how to discover the real problem, how to build, how to pitch, and how to close.

During the hackathon, switch to the **Hack** tab. Use it as a live checklist so the team does not forget the basics while everyone is tired, coding, pitching, eating too little, and making fast decisions.

The point is not to follow a script blindly. The point is to make sure the team keeps doing the important things when the event gets messy.

## Who This Is From

The guidebook was written by [Ralf Boltshauser](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ralfboltshauser/), based on repeated hackathon experience and shared learnings from the teams involved.

The core learnings came from work with Samuel Baumgartner, Marco Pagano, and Lionel Ding across multiple hackathons. Konrad te Heesen, Lukas Strickler, Marco Ginanesini, and Franz Dieter then extended the guidebook with additional experience, critique, and their own point of view.
